As often, there is no black and white. I agree with the statement in the sense that you are probably going to be more happy by studying subjects you are interested in. On the other hand, by chosing study subjects you have to keep in mind your chances on the labour market. That is simply because in modern society, people deine themselfs to a great extend through their work. Hence it makes little sense to study extravagant subjects when you have low chances of finding a job afterwards. Nevertheless, you shouldn't study somethig you truly dislike because you are going to be unhappy for many years of your professional life, maybe even for your whole life time. Plus, by working on something you don't realy feel comfortable with you most likely won't perform well enough anyway to do a "great career" in the common sense. Why not combine elements of interests and elements of job and career planning in your study schedule? Often there are varieties within the study subjects themself. When you study law and you are interested in history, take a course in legal history or even attend a history course or two at the other faculty. There is no obstacle to use your semester breaks by doing something totally different, like taking summer courses in other subjects or to go abroad for an art class. I just think that one should keep in mind a goal for one's professional life and constantly work towards that goal. That doesn't mean to get rid of your intellectual interests yet to adjust your study  schedule to some extent to the needs of the job market. Being without career opportunities after your favourite studies won't make a happy person out of you after all.

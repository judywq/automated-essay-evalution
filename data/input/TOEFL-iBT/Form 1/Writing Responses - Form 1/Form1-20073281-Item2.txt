I would agree with the statement that one should choose subjects of interest as opposed to just the vocational ones.
    It is indeed important the one needs to learn and polish the skills that would help to earn a livelihood but it is even more important that one does a job that he or she enjoys; so that in the process of earning three good meals a day, he/she is also living a happy and contended life. Such a life in my opinion is a sumptuous meal for the mind and the soul.
    Every person has certain traits that are natural to them. Not everybody who wishes to play a piano, for instance, can learn to play it to perfection. Instead, he or she may be more suited to taking up sports activities and excel at them. 
    I would also like to bring forth a personal case to exemplify my idea. I have a  cousin who has a natural ear to music and picks up playing a new musical instrument with relative ease. Her mother, however, was of the opinion that music was fine as a hobby but she had to do serious things like study subjects pertaining to physical sciences to be able to secure a career path. After a lot of persuasion from my cousin and her music teacher, my aunt gave in to her pleas of letting her take up studies in classical music. That decision would indeed make my aunt proud today when she sees her daughter as a respected music critic with a leading magazine in town. On the other hand, I have seen friends struggle with medical science subjects they have taken up owing to peer and family pressure. Most of them often drop out.
     What I want to point out is that studying what one is interested in should be of primary importance and a career, more often than not, sprouts forth from your excellence. In today's competitive world, it does not serve any purpose if one goes to school and parrots what is important for the markets today, rather to pick up subject that you like and you think you are good at and make your own career out of it.

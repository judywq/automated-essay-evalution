In the lecture, the professor stated that al three theories available are not wholly right to explain for a bird's ability to navigate.
The first theory that celestial objects help the bird navigate is limited. It is because the Sun and the stars are not always visible from the view of a flying bird. Some time when there are clouds, the clouds may hide the Sun and the stars so a bird cannot not use the celestial objects to find directions.
The second theory suggesting that landmarks are used by birds to navigate is also not sufficient. If birds relied entirely on landmarks such as rivers and coastlines, they would be unlikely to return to where they had started their journey. The memory of a bird does allow it to keep in mind specific landmarks but it does not mean that it can help the bird find the right way as many places have similar landmarks.
The third theory pointing out  that a bird uses an internal compass to respond to the Earth's magnetic field also have limitations. It's one thing that a bird can "feel' the magnetic field, but it is another thing that a bird can use their perception of the magnetic filed to navigate in their migrating journey. A bird has to find an answer to some questions such as "Where am I " and "How far is it until I reach the destination", and it is unlikely that magnetic field can help it answer these questions.
The explanation for the birds' ability to navigate may be a combination of all three theories stated above. It depends on the situation and a bird can use celestial objects, landmarks or Earth's magnatic field in different circumstances.

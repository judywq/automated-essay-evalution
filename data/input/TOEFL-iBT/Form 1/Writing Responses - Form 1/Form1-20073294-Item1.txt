	The lecture states that while all three theories for explaining birds' superb navigation abilities do have some support, none of them is sufficient to holistically explain this phenomenon. Each theory has its limitations and, therefore, can only be a partial explanation, but "not the whole story", as the professor put it.
	The first theory is criticized because the celestial objects are not always visible: the Sun and the stars are sometimes covered by clouds and, therefore, become invisible. However, birds are still able to find their way to their destination in spite of that. So this theory is not always true. There must be something else.
	The second theory is not always valid either. Even though birds do use their powerful spatial memory extensively in their migration journeys, that does not explain why birds fly to new places and still can find their way back to their nests or "know" their destination.
	Finally, the third theory has its limitations too. While having a "built-in" compass is a great idea, in order to use that compass effectively birds first need to "know" where they are at the beginning of the journey and, apparently, the direction in which this journey must be undertaken.

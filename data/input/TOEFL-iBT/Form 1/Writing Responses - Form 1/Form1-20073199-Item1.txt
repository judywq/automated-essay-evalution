None of the three theories outlined in the reading passage offers a sufficient explanation of the birds' ability to navigate over long distances. Given that they are not contradictory, one might take them as partial explanations of the phenomenon. Let us review the reasons why each of these theories appears unsufficient.

The first one faces the obvious counterexample of birds  not losing their navigatory skills when the weather conditions prevent them from seeing celestial objects. This implies that, although the ability to 'read' the sky as a map might be part of the general competence, it is not a necerssary one.

The second theory, claimimg that birds find their way by memorizing geographical features they encounter along their journeys, appears also unsufficient. If it were true, birds wouldn't be able to return home after being transfered to places they do not know. However, even under those conditions, birds do not suffer any loss in their orientation abilities, which clearly indicates that memorization of landmarks is not a necessary condition for birds to navigate accurately.

Finally, the theory that links the phenomenon to the presence of magnetite (a mineral that is sensible to the earth's magnetic field) in the birds' beaks also looks incomplete. Even if birds are thus able to determine which direction they fly (e.g. eastward or westward) this will give them no clue as to what their current position is. To navigate effectively, you need both a compass (that gives you direction) and a device that tells you where you are (take for example our modern GPS systems); the theory here only accounts for the first device, leaving the positioning problem unexplained.

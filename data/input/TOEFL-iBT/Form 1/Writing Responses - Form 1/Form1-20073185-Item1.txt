There are three theories which suggest how birds might navigate when performing their long migrations. However none of the theories are sufficiently comprehensive, each having its own limitations.

The first theory says that the birds use celestial object like the sun and the stars to navigate. They use the position of the objects to set their course. But the limitation to this theory is that it overlooks the fact that these celestial objects are not always visible, for example clouds may cover the sun or stars from vision. How will the birds navigate if they cannot see the stars and sun and do not know the direction?

Another theory is about memorizing landmarks. This theory suggests that birds use their memory to navigate. The hippocampal region of the birds brain picks out landmarks and memorises them to later use them to navigate. But this theory may not always apply because it has been observed that birds navigate perfectly even when they are taken to places they are not familiar with. Birds which are taken to completely unknown places, where they don't know any landmarks can always navigate and find their way back. This leaves room for other theories that might explains birds' navigational abilities.

The last theory proposed is that birds have a kind of internal compass embedded in their beaks. It is said to be a crystal of mineral magnetite which corresponds to the earths magnetic field, proposing that birds can sense this pull and navigate using the field. However just the orientation of a magnetic field is not sufficient to get yourself to a particular place. At least a sense of the starting place and anticipated destination is needed. An internal compass may provide a direction such as south or north, but it can't lead the birds to the exact place they want to go to.

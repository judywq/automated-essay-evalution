
The issue presented in the statement is about whether you should folow your heart or your brain in choosing your subjects of study.
This issue is important because depending on your choice, you may find your resume containg a lot of  preferences of subject in areas that ar less related to your career, or you may find your resume listing an array of subjects relevant to your carreer but for which you have low interested in working.

 I believe that  a balance between both is the right choice. You must be able to choose subjects that you are interested and you must be able to adapt to those subjects that are not interesting but  are important to your career. Therefore, I disagree with the statment.

One example of how it is important to balance interest and practibility  is a person who decides to be a sport coach. Let's assume that sport strategy is the real passion of this person, it would be safe also to assume that he/she will choose only subjects directly related to the game action like: how to best distribute the players in the field, what physical treats consider to certain positions, etc. 

Nevertheless, a coach does not deal with players in the field only. Generally, a coach's job is also about relationships. Coaches interact  with parents, school administrators, fans and  media reports, to name a few. If he/she chooses only game related subjects, he/she will not be learning about relationships. Because a significant part of a coach career is in dealing with other people, subjects on relationship and/or communication are fundamental for a successfull coach career.

 Even these might not be his/her primary interested, he/she will be able to better enjoy a chosen career if he/she is able to adapt and take subjects in all relevant fields of the profession.

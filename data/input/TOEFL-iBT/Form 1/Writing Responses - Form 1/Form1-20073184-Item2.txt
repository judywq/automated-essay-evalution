I comptetly agree with the statement. To me, the first question a student has to ask himself before choosing to study one particular subject is "Does it really interest me ?". If the answer is "Yes", then he can consider how relevant this subject is for his study program.
Of course, it is important to have a well-prepared study program that can lead to some career's oportunities.
But the life's too short to fell asleep in uninteresting courses. Moreover, life's quite full enough of stupid and borring things like insurance health declaration or sweden movies (but it's a personal point of view), so one absolutely doesn't need to add more of these things, like horrible, but "very useful" economic courses.
I like applied mathematics and mechanics mainly. I once made the mistake of enrolling a seminar of econometry, because others students in my school were telling that was a useful one. I immediatly dropped, and took a good course of computational fluid dynamics, which was really interesting.
There are, in theory, enough advisors and counselors in a university to help students in planning their future by choosing relevant courses. But what they do love is the question they must answer alone. It is not an easy one; some students love everything, others haven't discovered their way yet, others still hesitate between two major subjects...
Their field, their favorite subject is the only one that can give them the strength of doing all-nighter, the will to continue their studies and the energy theirs jobs will require each morning in the future.
So instead of thinking how much they will earn by choosing a "strategic" major right now, more students should ask themselves how borring will be the meetings with the committee of their audit company (but, of course, it's still examples from my own).

The three theories of birds' orientation during migration suggest navigation with the aid of celestial objects, landmarks, and magnetic crystals in their beaks working like a compass. None of these three hypotheses alone can explain the navigation skills of birds.
The sun and the stars are not always visible due to clouds. If birds relied on this visual information alone, they would be lost on a cloudy day or in a cloudy night. However, they find their destination despite such weather conditions.
Also, if landarks were the only aids for a bird's orientation, birds released in unkown locations would not find their way back to known territory. But tests have shown that they will.
Finally, magnetite crystals function purely as a compass, i.e. giving information about magnetic fields, i.e. directions, not about an absolute position. Therefore, birds would still need to have information about their current location in order to know which in direction they need to move and how far they need to fly.

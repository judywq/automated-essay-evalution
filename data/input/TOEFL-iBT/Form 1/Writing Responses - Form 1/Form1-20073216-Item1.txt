The lecture raises some questions about the valability of the three mentioned theories in the text. The method the professor uses for this purpose, is by addressing several limitations of the the three theories. In other words, he partially agrees to all three theories, but admits that these theories not always explain how birds are able to orientate. 

To begin with, the lack that the professor finds in the first theory, the one that assumes that birds navigate by orientating after the celestial objects, occurs in situations where the celestial objects are not visible because of the clouds. Nevertheless, the birds are able to navigate also in these conditions. Therefore, it is normal to assume that the birds must have another elements that help them know where they are and where they are heading to.

The second theory presented in the text assumes that birds navigate by following landmarks, like: rivers, coastlines or mountains. The flaw that the professor sees in this theory is that, if the birds were to orientate only after these landmarks, they wouldn't be able to find their way back home when being in a new location. Moreover, in support of this opinion comes a study in which birds were released in a new location. According to the study, the released birds could find their way back home without knowing exactly where they were. 

Lastly, what the third theory is concerned, the professor believes that the internal compass of the birds is not enough to help them navigate. In order to be able to use the "built in compass" they must know where they are before deciding where they should be heading to. For this reason, the internal compass cannot solely account for the ability of the migrating birds to navigate.

To sum up, the professor mainly agrees the three theories presented in the text. However, he believes that other elements in addition to these help the migrating birds navigate.

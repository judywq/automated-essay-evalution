According to the lecture there are three benefits to commercial fish production:

Firstly, fish farming increases the amount of fish avaible for the consumer market, while giving the wild fish time to recoup from years of overfishing.  While there is a small risk that the commercially grown fish could cause disease to spread among the wild fish in the area, this risk is outweighed by the opportunity for the wild fish to increase in number, while being protected from commercial overfishing.

Secondly, the chemicals used to grow bigger fish in a short time, as practised on some fish farms, are the same type of growth hormone used by the beef, poultry and pork industry.  One should therefore compare commercially grown fish to these other types of food, and not to the wild fish they are bred to replace on the dinner table. In other words, commercially produced fish pose no  greater risk to human health than commercially produced beef or poultry.

Thirdly, feeding commercially grown fish with fish meal is not as wasteful as it might appear, because the fish used in the making of the fish meal are usually not eaten by humans.  By using meal from these inedible fish, commercial fisheries are in fact increasing the amount of edible fish available to consumers.

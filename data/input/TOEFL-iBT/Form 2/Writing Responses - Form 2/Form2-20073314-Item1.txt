The lecturer goes through writer's points and gives counterarguments against each one.
The writer's first argument against fish-farming is its danger to the fish in the surrounding areas. While the writer focusses on the danger that comes from deseases that can develop easily in small enclosures holding many fish, the lecturer argues that the benefits for the local fish population are more important. 
Since the fish-farmers take care of a part of the populations food production needs, less fish will be hunted in the traditional way. 
The next point deals with the possible effects on humans when eating "farmed" fish. The writer compares these fish that sometimes get fed growth-inducing chemicals with wild fish,  the lecturer argues that it makes more sense to compare it to other kinds of food.
Meat and other food that we consume daily includes more of these chemicals than fish from a fish-farm. Therefore he argues that for the consumer it's still a pretty good deal.
The third and last point deals with the "productivity" of fish-farming. Each pound of fish that is raised in a farm consumes many times his own weight in fish meal. The lecturer argues that most of the fish that are processed into fish meal are non-edible. Since they are of  no use to humans, he claims the process of using non-edible fish into edible fish as very effective.

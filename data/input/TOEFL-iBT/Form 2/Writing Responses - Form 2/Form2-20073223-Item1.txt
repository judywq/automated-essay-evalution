The lecture challenges every three points that build up the argumentation against firsh farming in the reading passage. Nowadays, over thirty percent of the fish consumed is grown in farms, but the consequences of the increasing use of fish-farming may not be as harmful as the reading passage suggests.

First, although fish growing in farms may develop diseases and parasitic infections, the spreading impact of these infections on wild fish is rather negligeable in comparison to the consequences of commercial fishing:  the practice of commercial fishing has endangered the survival of a lot of coastal fish species; these species could be saved through fish farming, which allows a large number of fishes to grow and be sustained in a specific area.

Second, the risk of growth-inducing chemicals to human consumers is comparable to the one coming from poultry, beef or pork farming.  In comparison to these animals, fish represents a very healthy alternative, since it has less fat.

Third, the long-term wastefulness induced with the process of fish farming is largely undermined by the nature of fishes that constitutes meals: these fishes will not end up on human plates anyway, therefore it is a rather positive solution to recycle these non-eatable fishes into fish-farming food.

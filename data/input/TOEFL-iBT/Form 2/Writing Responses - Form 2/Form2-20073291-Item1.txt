The reading passage describes three problems of fish farming. In the lecture, each problem is adressed and critisized. 
  The first problem is that of diseases and parasites contaminating wild fish. The lecturer makes the obvious point that for that to happen, there must be wild fish in the first place. In most cases, there aren't, he claims. They have disappeared because of traditional commercial fishing. Far from being harmful, fish farming provides a helfpul alternative to traditional fishing.
  The second problem described in the text is that of risks to human consumers, resulting from the growth-inducing chemicals used in fish farming. The lecturer claims that this argument is biased. Instead of comparing farm fish with wild fish, one should compare them to poultry, pork and beef, which are also stuffed with all kinds of chemicals. Then the conclusion will be that farm fish is healthier. 
  Finally, the lecturer adresses the problem of using fish to feed fish in farms, whitch the text described as wasteful. He argues that the author of the text has overlooked the fact that it concernes different species. The fish used for fish meal are not edible for humans. Using them in this way means that more species can be used for human food production. 
  The conclusion implied - but not stated - is that fish farming is benificial rather that harmful, both to humans and to the environment.
